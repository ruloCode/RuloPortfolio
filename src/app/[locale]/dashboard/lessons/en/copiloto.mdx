## The problem

You ask AI for things like it's Google. And it answers like Google: generic, correct, useless.

"Write me an email for a client" produces an email that works for any client in the world — which means for none. You read it, you don't like it, you rewrite it yourself. You lost two minutes and ended up in the same place.

The problem isn't the model. It's that you gave it 5% of the context you have in your head.

## The 4 parts of a prompt that works

A prompt that works has four parts. Always the same ones:

1. **Role** — who it is. Not decorative: it changes the vocabulary and the priorities of the answer.
2. **Context** — what it needs to know that only you know. This is where almost everyone falls short.
3. **Task** — what it has to do, in one sentence.
4. **Format** — how it has to come out. Don't say it and it comes out however, and you fix it yourself.

## Your base prompt

Copy this and fill it with task #1 from your inventory:

```text
You are [ROLE: e.g. the assistant to an operations consultant who
writes to corporate clients in Colombia].

Context:
- About me: [what you do, for whom]
- About the reader: [who they are, what they care about, what they know]
- How I write: [direct / warm / formal — and what you NEVER say]
- Constraints: [length, things you can't promise]

Task: [one sentence. What you want it to do.]

Output format:
- [e.g. 120 words maximum]
- [e.g. no generic openers like "hope you're doing well"]
- [e.g. always end with a concrete question]

If you're missing information to do this well, ask me before writing.
```

That last line does more than it looks. Without it, the model invents what it doesn't know. With it, it asks you — and the answer comes out right the first time.

## Test it now

Not later. Take task #1 from your inventory and run it.

First pass: the answer will be 70% there. That's normal.

## When it fails and how you fix it

Two iterations, not twenty:

- **If the tone is wrong** → don't say "more casual". Give it an example of yours: "this is how I write:" and paste something real you wrote. One example beats five adjectives.
- **If the content is wrong** → context is missing. Go back to section 2 and add what you assumed was obvious.
- **If the format is wrong** → be more specific. "Short" isn't an instruction. "120 words maximum" is.

If it's still wrong on the third iteration, the problem is the task has no clear rules. Go back to lesson 1 — it probably wasn't a candidate.

## Where you save it

A prompt living in a chat you closed doesn't exist.

Save it where you already work: a pinned document, a note, a saved project in your AI tool. The requirement isn't the tool — it's that tomorrow you find it in under ten seconds. Longer and you'll rewrite it from scratch, and we're back to the start.

## Your turn

Tomorrow you open it and reuse it. That's what having a copilot means: not the tool, the prompt.
